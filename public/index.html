What is Hijama Cupping Therapy?

When exploring the concept of 'Cupping,' it's essential to discern the specific type, as they vary significantly in method and outcome. There exist primarily TWO variants of Cuppingâ€”Blood Cupping and Dry Cupping. Blood Cupping, often termed Wet Cupping, involves the withdrawal of blood from the body (such as in Hijama). On the contrary, Dry Cupping doesn't involve blood removal and has gained visibility, notably among Olympic athletes, identifiable by the circular marks it imprints on the skin. Recognizing this distinction is pivotal for a comprehensive understanding of Cupping practices.

How Blood Cupping Works?

The process of Blood Cupping involves employing a surgical tool to gently scrape or pierce the skin, creating an entry point for blood extraction. Subsequently, a cup is strategically applied to gather the extracted blood. The air within the cup is then systematically eliminated, generating a vacuum effect. This vacuum, in turn, facilitates the drawing out of blood from the body, accumulating it within the confines of the cup. Throughout centuries, the techniques employed to create this vacuum have undergone diverse adaptations. Simultaneously, the materials utilized for crafting the cup have also witnessed substantial variations. The evolution of both vacuum-forming methods and cup materials reflects the historical diversity within the practice of Blood Cupping.

How Dry Cupping Works?

Cups are strategically positioned on different body points, generating a vacuum within. These cups are left undisturbed for a duration, allowing the skin to swell under the influence of the vacuum, with no blood extraction involved. Diverse materials, including glass, plastic, bamboo, or silicone, can be utilized for crafting these cups. The claim suggests that skin swelling, induced by the vacuum, may enhance blood circulation and alleviate issues like swelling and tension. It's crucial to emphasize that this practice differs significantly from the traditional Hijama, where blood extraction is a fundamental aspect.

Hijama

The term 'Hijama' in Arabic is rooted in the word 'hajm,' denoting the action of sucking. It specifically pertains to the practice of Blood Cupping. Muslim Cupping therapists typically undergo specialized training that centers on the Sunnah Cupping points situated on the body. These specific points, located between the shoulder blades and slightly towards the neck on the back, are integral to traditional Hijama practices. While numerous Cupping points exist, each designed to address specific conditions, individuals seeking Hijama for Sunnah adherence can anticipate therapists applying cups exclusively on these designated 'Sunnah points.'
